BP Tracker — Info

What the numbers mean + healthy ranges + how to lower BP

Quick Definitions

What is Systolic, Diastolic, Pulse?

Systolic (top number) is the pressure in your arteries when your heart beats.

Diastolic (bottom number) is the pressure when your heart rests between beats.

Pulse is your heart rate (beats per minute).

Healthy ranges (general guidance)

Targets vary by age/conditions and your clinician’s advice. This is a general guide for adults:

Normal <120 systolic and <80 diastolic
Elevated 120–129 systolic and <80 diastolic
High (Stage 1) 130–139 systolic or 80–89 diastolic
High (Stage 2) ≥140 systolic or ≥90 diastolic
Urgent ≥180 systolic and/or ≥120 diastolic

If you feel unwell (chest pain, severe headache, shortness of breath, weakness, vision changes), seek urgent care.

How to take BP correctly

Before you measure
  • Rest quietly for 5 minutes beforehand.
  • Avoid caffeine, smoking, exercise for ~30 minutes beforehand.
  • Empty your bladder.
  • Use the correct cuff size (too small = higher readings).
During the measurement
  • Sit with back supported, feet flat, legs uncrossed.
  • Arm supported at heart level.
  • Place cuff on bare upper arm (not over clothing).
  • Don’t talk or move while measuring.
3 readings rule (what your app does)
  • Take 3 readings, about 1 minute apart.
  • Record all three and use the average for trend tracking.
  • Try to measure at the same time daily for consistency.

How to lower BP

High-impact habits
  • Reduce salt (especially processed foods).
  • Walk / cardio most days (even 20–30 min).
  • Lose a little weight if needed (even small changes help).
  • Sleep 7–9 hours; treat snoring/sleep apnea if relevant.
  • Limit alcohol; stop smoking.
  • Manage stress (breathing, routines, consistent exercise).
Foods that support lower BP
  • Vegetables (leafy greens), fruits (berries, bananas), beans/legumes.
  • Oats, whole grains, nuts/seeds (watch salt).
  • Low-fat dairy or calcium-rich alternatives.
  • Fish (omega-3), olive oil, unsalted nuts.
  • Potassium-rich foods can help (unless you’ve been told to limit potassium).
When to talk to a clinician
  • If your average readings are consistently in the high ranges.
  • If you have symptoms (chest pain, severe headache, breathlessness, weakness, vision changes).
  • If your device or technique might be off — bring your monitor to check accuracy.
